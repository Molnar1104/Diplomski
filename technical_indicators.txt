### Simple Moving Average (SMA)

**Concept:**
A Simple Moving Average (SMA) is a core technical indicator that smooths out price data to create a single flowing line, making it easier to identify the direction of the trend. It is calculated by summing the closing prices of a security over a specific number of periods (e.g., 10, 50, or 200 days) and then dividing by that number of periods.

**Interpretation:**
- **Trend Identification:** When the price is consistently above the SMA, it is generally considered an uptrend. Conversely, a price consistently below the SMA suggests a downtrend.
- **Support and Resistance:** Longer-term SMAs (like the 50-day or 200-day) are often watched as key levels of support or resistance.
- **Crossovers:** When a short-term SMA (e.g., 50-day) crosses above a long-term SMA (e.g., 200-day), it is known as a "golden cross" and is often seen as a bullish signal. The opposite, a "death cross," is a bearish signal.

### Relative Strength Index (RSI)

**Concept:**
The Relative Strength Index (RSI) is a momentum oscillator that measures the speed and change of price movements. It oscillates between zero and 100. The RSI is calculated using the average gain and average loss over a specified period, typically 14 days.

**Interpretation:**
- **Overbought/Oversold:** The RSI is primarily used to identify overbought or oversold conditions in a market.
  - An RSI reading above 70 is often considered overbought, suggesting that the asset may be due for a price correction.
  - An RSI reading below 30 is often considered oversold, suggesting that the asset may be due for a rebound.
- **Divergence:** Divergence occurs when the price makes a new high or low, but the RSI does not. For example, if the price makes a new high but the RSI makes a lower high, it is a bearish divergence and can signal a potential price reversal.

### Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD)

**Concept:**
The Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD) is a trend-following momentum indicator that shows the relationship between two exponential moving averages (EMAs) of a securityâ€™s price. The MACD is calculated by subtracting the 26-period EMA from the 12-period EMA. A nine-period EMA of the MACD, called the "signal line," is then plotted on top of the MACD line, which can function as a trigger for buy and sell signals.

**Interpretation:**
The MACD is composed of three main components:
1.  **MACD Line:** The difference between the 12-period and 26-period EMAs.
2.  **Signal Line:** The 9-period EMA of the MACD line.
3.  **Histogram:** The difference between the MACD line and the signal line.

Key signals from the MACD include:
- **Crossovers:** A bullish signal occurs when the MACD line crosses above the signal line. A bearish signal occurs when the MACD line crosses below the signal line.
- **Centerline Crossovers:** A bullish signal occurs when the MACD line crosses above the zero line. A bearish signal occurs when it crosses below the zero line.
- **Divergence:** Similar to the RSI, divergence between the price and the MACD can signal a potential reversal.
